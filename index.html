How We Build

Engineering‑Driven, Practical By Design

Our product development is backed by more than a decade of real engineering and manufacturing experience, including process engineering in electronics manufacturing and hands‑on sheet metal fabrication. That means we think in terms of flow, repeatability, and how things actually get made and used — not just how they look in a render.

Lean manufacturing principles, fixture design, and process control all shape how we approach these workstations. We use proven CAD tools and in‑house 3D printing to move fast: build, test, adjust, repeat. Parts don’t just look good on a screen — they get handled, abused, and refined until they feel right in real use.

Who I Am

The Person Behind Momma’s Pop Shop

I worked my way through school doing custom framing, learning how to measure twice, cut once, and care about the details people actually see every day. From there, I moved into a long‑term role in a not‑for‑profit insurance program that helps people who struggle to get coverage in the standard market.

We do the work of a full insurance company with a lean team, which means staying organized, focused, and efficient every single day. Along the way, I earned a CPCU (Chartered Property Casualty Underwriter), a high‑level insurance and risk designation, and later moved into accounting work.

That mix of hands‑on craftsmanship, financial discipline, and serving people who don’t always have easy options is what I bring into this business. I care about details, I care about numbers, and I care about building products that make people’s day‑to‑day lives easier, not harder.

How That Shapes This Business

The business side is grounded in long‑term, not‑for‑profit work and formal training in risk and accounting. The product side is driven by more than ten years of engineering and manufacturing experience in electronics assembly, process improvement, and fabrication.

Together, that combination means we think about both sides of every decision: does it work for the person using it, and does it make sense for the business that has to build it? The result is a small company that treats quality, process, and practicality like they actually matter.
